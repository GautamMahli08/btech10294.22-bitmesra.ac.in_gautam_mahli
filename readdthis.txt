Designing a One‑Minute, Source‑Backed Medical Q&A Assistant for Doctors
Executive overview
Doctors increasingly need concise, trustworthy answers at the point of care but have limited time to search or read long articles. Modern large language models (LLMs) can reach near‑expert performance on exam‑style medical questions, yet they still hallucinate and often cite sources that do not actually support their claims. The most realistic goal is not “zero hallucination” but a system that (1) constrains itself to high‑quality medical content, (2) is architected for citation and verifiability, and (3) keeps a clinician firmly in the loop for judgment and final decisions.
Commercial tools such as DynaMed’s Dyna AI and UpToDate Expert AI show that point‑of‑care assistants can answer natural‑language clinical questions in under a minute while providing direct links into curated reference content. Research on hallucination mitigation in medical LLMs highlights the importance of retrieval‑augmented generation (RAG), domain‑specific fine‑tuning, automated fact‑checking and human oversight to improve factuality and safety. Building on these patterns, a practical system for doctors should combine a curated medical knowledge base, a retrieval layer, a generation and verification layer, and a workflow‑aware interface optimized for 30–60 second interactions.
Why pure LLMs are not enough
Benchmarks such as MedQA (USMLE) show that advanced models like Med‑PaLM 2 and GPT‑4‑class systems can achieve over 80% accuracy on multiple‑choice medical questions, outperforming many earlier domain‑specific models. However, these scores reflect performance on carefully constructed test sets, not the open‑ended, messy questions doctors ask in real clinics. Studies comparing LLM answers to physician answers on consumer medical queries find that AI can be highly articulate and even preferred on empathy or clarity, but this does not guarantee that every fact is correct or that sources are traceable for medico‑legal purposes.
Recent work introducing the SourceCheckup framework evaluated seven popular LLMs on 800 medical questions and found that between 50% and 90% of responses were not fully supported by the cited references. Even GPT‑4o with web search had around 30% of individual statements unsupported and nearly half of responses not fully supported by their cited sources. Separate surveys of hallucination mitigation techniques emphasize that hallucination remains a significant, unsolved problem in knowledge‑intensive domains like medicine despite improvements from retrieval, decoding tweaks, and alignment methods. These findings indicate that a clinical Q&A assistant must be designed around constraints and verification rather than relying on a general LLM alone.
Patterns from existing clinical decision support tools
The AI features of leading clinical decision support tools provide useful blueprints. DynaMed’s Dyna AI uses a RAG framework that restricts retrieval to DynaMed, DynaMedex and Dynamic Health, which are expert‑curated, evidence‑based resources updated from more than 250 journals and containing over 100,000 citations. When a clinician asks a question, Dyna AI retrieves relevant guideline summaries and expert commentary, synthesizes a concise answer, and highlights which topic subsections were used, with one‑click access for verification. This closed content loop prevents the model from pulling unvetted material from the open internet and clarifies source attribution.
UpToDate Expert AI similarly grounds its answers exclusively in UpToDate’s proprietary, peer‑reviewed topics and provides direct links to supporting sections, along with visible assumptions and reasoning. Comparative analyses of clinical decision support tools in 2025–2026 describe a “provenance‑first” trend where generative AI is layered on top of established evidence libraries, rather than used as a standalone oracle. Outside the proprietary ecosystem, tools like OpenEvidence emphasize literature‑powered answers for US physicians, again tightly linking AI‑generated summaries to underlying trial data and guidelines. These systems demonstrate that fast, citation‑rich answers are achievable when the AI is embedded in a curated environment.
Key requirements for a one‑minute doctor assistant
A realistic one‑minute assistant for doctors must satisfy several technical and product requirements:
Strict content boundaries. All clinical statements should be grounded in vetted sources: major guidelines, peer‑reviewed systematic reviews, trusted disease compendia, and high‑quality drug references, similar to the content bases of DynaMed and UpToDate.
Retrieval‑augmented generation (RAG). The model should retrieve a small set of highly relevant passages for each query and be explicitly instructed to answer using only those passages, a method widely recognized as central to hallucination reduction.
Transparent citations. Every key recommendation or fact should be linked inline to one or more underlying documents, mirroring how Dyna AI and UpToDate Expert AI surface topic sections and references in their chat interfaces.
Latency budget. The system must deliver an initial summary within roughly 10–20 seconds and complete the full response within about one minute on typical hardware, which is consistent with expectations for point‑of‑care decision support tools.
Human‑in‑the‑loop role. The assistant should be positioned as a tool to support, not replace, clinician judgment; hallucination mitigation frameworks repeatedly stress the importance of expert oversight in high‑stakes settings.
System architecture overview
A high‑level architecture for such an assistant can be decomposed into four main layers: data, retrieval, generation and verification, and user interface. Each layer can be designed to reduce hallucinations and shorten time‑to‑answer.
Data and knowledge base layer
The foundation is a well‑maintained medical knowledge base that prioritizes recency and evidentiary quality. Commercial tools license or own curated content libraries; a custom system could combine open or institutional sources such as:
National and international guidelines (for example, WHO, NICE, specialty societies), which are commonly used as standards of care.
Structured disease and drug compendia or textbooks, analogous in role to resources like DynaMedex or Micromedex.
PubMed‑indexed clinical trials and systematic reviews, with emphasis on high‑level evidence such as randomized controlled trials and meta‑analyses.
Documents should be chunked into semantically coherent sections and tagged by specialty, condition, intervention, population, and recency. Continuous monitoring and ingestion pipelines similar to those used by DynaMed, which tracks over 250 journals to keep more than 100,000 citations current, help ensure the assistant reflects up‑to‑date knowledge.
Retrieval and ranking layer
On top of this corpus, a retrieval layer selects a small set of passages that are most relevant to the clinician’s question. Reviews of hallucination mitigation techniques describe retrieval‑augmented generation as one of the most effective strategies for knowledge‑intensive tasks, particularly when combined with careful index design. In practice, a hybrid of lexical retrieval (for example, BM25) and dense vector search (for example, transformer‑based embeddings) can capture both exact medical terms and semantic similarity.
Filters by specialty, patient age group, and publication date help narrow results quickly; for point‑of‑care use, recency filters that favor guidelines and reviews from the last 3–5 years are especially important in fast‑changing areas. The retrieval stage should be fast—typically under a second for a handful of top passages—so that most of the latency budget is available to the generator.
Generation and citation layer
The generation layer uses an LLM to synthesize a concise answer from the retrieved context. Research on medical LLMs highlights several patterns for safer generation:
Domain specialization. Fine‑tuning or instruction‑tuning a base model on high‑quality medical QA datasets and clinical notes reduces generic hallucinations and improves familiarity with terminology.
Context‑bounded prompting. The model should be explicitly instructed to answer only from the provided passages, to quote rather than invent numbers, and to say when the evidence is unclear or missing.
Structured outputs. Templates that separate “Summary,” “Differential diagnosis,” “Investigations,” “Management options,” and “Evidence and guideline links” help keep answers focused and quickly scannable.
For citations, the model can be guided to attach inline links or reference markers to the specific passages it drew from. SourceCheckup and related work show that naïve citation prompting is insufficient—LLMs often attach incorrect sources—so a more robust approach is to compute citations deterministically from the retrieval step or to post‑process the draft answer using automated source‑checking.
Verification and hallucination mitigation layer
Multiple studies propose additional safeguards on top of RAG to reduce hallucinations in healthcare settings. A scoping review of mitigation techniques emphasizes layered defenses: retrieval constraints, decoding strategies, self‑consistency checks, and post‑hoc fact‑checking. Medical‑specific work on granular fact‑checking describes pipelines where claims are extracted from the generated summary, matched back to the source corpus, and flagged if contradicted or unsupported.
Other research introduces contrastive decoding and ensemble approaches that compare generations from different models or different decoding settings to identify unstable or unsupported statements. Human‑in‑the‑loop frameworks recommend routing high‑risk outputs—such as drug dosing advice for narrow therapeutic index drugs or rare conditions—to stricter review flows, possibly including real‑time clinician oversight. Practically, a one‑minute assistant could implement fast automated checks and display confidence indicators or warnings when evidence is weak or conflicting.
User experience and workflow design for busy doctors
Even a technically robust system will fail if it does not fit into clinicians’ time‑pressed workflows. Contemporary tools are moving toward conversational interfaces where clinicians can ask questions in natural language “as they would to a colleague,” then drill down via links into underlying topics for more detail. To meet a one‑minute constraint, the interface should prioritize:
Single‑screen summaries. The first answer block should be a short, actionable summary with 3–5 bullet points tailored to the question type (for example, diagnosis, next test, initial management), followed by collapsible detail sections.
Prominent source links. Direct links to guidelines, topic pages, and key trials should appear near each recommendation so the doctor can quickly validate or read further when necessary.
Fast follow‑ups. Chat‑style follow‑up questions (for example, “What if the patient has chronic kidney disease?”) should preserve context and return updated recommendations without the clinician needing to restate the entire case.
Latency can be further reduced by streaming partial answers: the summary appears within seconds while deeper reasoning and additional citations continue to load. Mobile and EHR‑integrated views help doctors access the assistant inline during documentation or order entry, which emerging AI clinical tools increasingly support.
What “accurate, not hallucinated” really means in practice
Given current evidence, promising systems can greatly reduce hallucinations but cannot eliminate them. Large surveys of hallucination mitigation techniques emphasize that even with RAG and alignment, models may still generate unsupported statements, particularly for rare conditions, out‑of‑distribution cases, or ambiguous questions. The SourceCheckup study underscores that citation presence is not the same as citation correctness; many LLMs attach seemingly plausible references that do not actually support the claims made.
Clinical frameworks therefore advocate for “responsible AI” rather than “infallible AI”: strict scope limitations, transparent uncertainty communication, and mandatory human oversight. In practical terms, a one‑minute assistant should:
Clearly label itself as an information and education tool, not a prescriber or autonomous diagnostic system.
Encourage users to verify critical recommendations via the provided links to guidelines and primary literature.
Provide explicit statements when evidence is limited or conflicting, instead of forcing a definitive recommendation.
Implementation milestones for building such a system
For teams designing a fast, citation‑rich medical assistant, the literature suggests a phased approach:
Assemble and normalize the corpus. Start with a well‑defined set of high‑quality sources (guidelines, disease references, drug compendia) and build ingestion pipelines with metadata tagging and periodic updates.
Implement a robust RAG stack. Combine lexical and vector search over chunked documents, with specialty and recency filters; instrument retrieval to support deterministic citation mapping.
Select and adapt an LLM. Evaluate strong general or medical‑tuned models on benchmarks such as MedQA and MedMCQA and perform domain‑specific instruction‑tuning if needed.
Add automated verification. Integrate fact‑checking modules that re‑match generated claims to the corpus, using techniques from granular fact‑checking and hallucination mitigation research.
Co‑design workflow with clinicians. Pilot the assistant with doctors, iterating on templates, safety constraints, and latency budgets to ensure answers are genuinely usable within one‑minute windows.
By following these patterns, a system can approximate the experience many clinicians want: ask a focused question, receive a clear, well‑structured answer in under a minute, and have immediate access to the underlying evidence to confirm or challenge what the AI suggests.
